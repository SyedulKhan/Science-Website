Higgs boson: From theory to discovery
Background

Back in the 1970s, scientists and researchers realised that there are correlations between two of the four fundamental forces; the electromagnetic force and the weak force. Those two forces were analysed into theory and formed the grounds of the Standard Model. This Model belongs to particle physics, a field driven by physicists dedicated to breaking down the endlessly complicated universe to its most fundamental building blocks. The relationship between the electromagnetic and weak force implied that electricity, magnetism, light and some types of radioactivity are all manifestations of a single underlying force known as the electroweak force.

The equations and theorems that described this force and its particles, the photon and the W and Z bosons, were all correct apart from a big problem; all these particles emerged without a mass. Now that would not be a problem for the photon, but the W and Z have mass. This is where theoretical physicists Robert Brout, Francois Englert and Peter Higgs came in to solve this problem. They suggested a mechanism, which took the name Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism afterwards, that gives a mass to the W and Z when they interact with an invisible field, now called the Higgs field, which fills the universe. The Higgs Field, like most fundamental fields, should have an associated particle – the Higgs boson. That boson, when found, would be a visible manifestation of the Higgs field.

Preparation

To implement and put in practise all these years of questioning, reasoning and theorems, a colossal source of experimental energy should exist to make such goals even observable. For this reason, but for many other as well, the famous Large Hadron Collider was built. For those that have not heard of it, Large Hadron Collider is a particle collider built by CERN. To construct this, it took 10 years and 10.000 scientists and engineers. Hence, one could argue that LHC is the most complex and sophisticated experimental facility ever built.

The collider is equipped with superconducting magnets and accelerating structures that are required to boost the speed of the colliding particles. In order to avoid the loss of energy and achieve minimal electrical resistance these magnets need to be cooled down to -271.3°C and for that reason the LHC is being supplied with liquid helium. The accelerator contains two pipes; each is filled with high-energy particle beams that are being accelerated in opposite directions until they reach a speed that is close to that of the speed of light. Making that happen requires keeping those two pipes at ultrahigh vacuum. Then the two beams, being under control, are made to collide.

Many experiments were conducted with no desired outcome throughout the starting years of LHC's function, but the scientists that work there reported helpful evidence every time.

Discovery

On the 4th of July 2012, two experiments that were being conducted at CERN’s LHC announced they had each observed a new particle that possesses a mass of approximately 126 giga-electron volts (1 GeV = 1 billion electron volts). This particle consisted with the Higgs boson that was predicted by the Standard Model.

A year later, Francois Englert and Peter Higgs were awarded with the Nobel prize in physics for their theoretical work that contributed in understanding the origin of mass of subatomic particles. You can see the nomination video here.

Considerations

There are researchers that argue that even though the Higgs boson that was discovered back in 2012 may indeed look like what they were looking for, it is not exactly that. Looking back to our history we have seen cases that theories became realities and remain so but we have also witnessed cases that theories were made into beliefs that lasted centuries and afterwards were disproved. The latter case though, was not caused by misguidance but because of insufficient evidence paired together with the non-perishable impatience of the humankind. In which categories of the two does the Higgs discovery belong to? Only a few might be able to really tell but even so it is too early to say. Not to mention that there are many other theorems related to Higgs boson that are waiting to be tested upon in LHC.

Finding such a particle or any particle for that matter is like a person that clapped too early in a special event; at that moment, nobody knows if it was the right action but that single clap could result to the whole audience clapping. Higgs boson or not, the people that are pursuing and devoting their lives to this, certainly deserve our gratitude for trying to solve one of the biggest questions of humankind.
